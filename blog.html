The Need For Alternative Mechanisms In Post- Election Dispute In Nigeria

  • Hagler Okorie
  • Post-Election
  • 14

Election is the act of selecting someone or voting to select the winner of a political office. It is the bedrock of representative democracy. In Ojukwu V. Obasanjo, the Supreme Court held that:
“…the word ‘election’ in the content in which it was used in Section 137 (1) (b) of the Constitution means a process of choosing by popular votes a candidate for political office in a democratic system of government”.

Also in APGA V. Ohakim, the Court of Appeal held that:
“The purpose of holding an election in a democratic set up is to determine the wishes of the people as to who should represent them in their legislative and executive set up”. The court went ahead to caution players and umpires to ensure that any such election exercise is done in order to substantially ensure the main objectives are practically met. Section 156 of the Electoral Act, 2010 (as amended) defines election to include “a referendum”.

In this regard the Court of Appeal in expanding this provision stated:
“The word ‘election’ is a generic term. It is a process which embraces the entire gamut of activities ranging from accreditation, voting, collation to recording on all relevant INEC forms and declaration of result”.

The same thread runs in the intestine of the decisions of the Court of Appeal in Fayemi V. Oni and P.P.A.V. Saraki, where the Courts held that this process by the Constitutional definition starts from voting by members to choosing candidates to represent the party at ward level and the primaries up to the polling day when these party candidates are presented by the party to the electorate.

THE ETIOLOGY OF ELECTION IN NIGERIA
Historically speaking, the principles and concept of election was introduced into our political landscape in 1922 though only for the Southern Nigeria. The 1946 Ordinance established Regional House of Assembly. The Governor was empowered to make electoral regulations through proclamations. The 1954 Constitution divided the Regions into Electoral districts and each district allocated a seat.

In 1958, there was the establishment of a House of Representative where all the regions would elect there representatives. This Order also created and established an “Electoral Commission” and demarcated the country into constituencies. The Regulation or Order contained provisions dealing with Election Petition.The High Court was for the first time, given jurisdiction to hear and determine election petitions. Appeals lie from the decisions of the High Court to the Supreme Court.

Upon the attainment of independence, the Nigeria Electoral (Transitional Provisions) Act 1901 was enacted. The Act validated the Elections (House of Representatives) Regulation 1958 and the Federal Legislative House (Dispute Seats) Regulations 1959. In 1962, there was what was termed, the first autonomous Electoral Law in the Country. This is the enactment by the Federal Parliament the “Electoral Act”. Part IV of this Act embodied elaborate provisions on election petition. In 1964, the Electoral (Amendment) Act was enacted. The Act abolished the requirement for payment of deposit on lodging of petition.

Later, there were the 1977 Electoral Decree amended in 1978 and 1979. In 1982, the National Assembly enacted a new Electoral Act which provisions are similar to the provisions of the 1977 Act. The Act established and mandated the Federal Electoral Commission to compile a new voter register. Election Petitions under this Act were vested on the Federal High Court, State High Court and High Court of the Federal Capital Territory. Appeals lie from the High Court to the Court of Appeal and to the Supreme Court. This Act guided the conduct of the 1983 general elections. After the suspension of the Electoral Act of 1982 by the General Buhari Military Government, there have been several others by different Military regimes until the present Electoral Act 2010 (as amended).

Under these various Electoral Acts, especially since after independence, there have been fiery legal contestations by different candidates and parties before either election courts or tribunals. These courts or tribunals have been almost liquidated and inundated by election petitions even to a breaking point. This results into the halting of the usual and normal business of the Court. Aside the delay in the delivery of decisions on normal court cases, our judge are so scandalized by these litigants over what they subjectively perceived to be an unfair judgment of the Court or tribunal. Another disfunction of election petition to our judicial system is the issue that it leads judges to use harsh language which uniquely contribute to overall negative civil discourse. The appellate courts in reversing some decisions of lower courts offer scathing criticisms and chastisement. Such tones or inflammatory statements damage the integrity of the judiciary and lessen the trust which the public places in judicial opinions. The majority of vituperative sarcastical comments flourish as if judges are exasperated with one another.

Our country is already deeply polarized on many political issues and when courts or tribunal decide election law cases, they are either determining the rules of the game or the ultimate outcome of an election. Thus courts decisions contribute to the overall national discussion of who should lead our country. So, when courts render these decisions with an overtly negative tone, they add to the preexisting caustic political discourse. The media repeat these sentiments when reporting on the decisions. Furthermore, the media usually identify the presumed political affiliation of the judge deciding the case, lending a tenor of perceived partisanship to the decision in the eyes of the public.

The Scenario pointed above was the position our country found itself in the general elections conducted in Nigeria in 1964, 1979, 1983, 1999, 2003, 2007, and 2011. The magnitude and forest of election petitions that arose from the 2007 general elections were unprecedented in the electoral history of this country. Election cases filed at various Election Tribunals and the court of Appeal were put at 1,290. Anambra State recorded the highest with 154 petitions, while Yobe State with 6 Petitions has the least. The Presidential election petitions were 8 in all. The nation’s legal system almost collapsed as a result of unnecessary emphasis on election litigations.
