A Critical Review of the Conduct of Non-International Armed Conflict in Nigeria and the Developmental Challenges Inherent

  • Hagler Okorie
  • Armed Conflicts
  • 14

Law of armed conflict is defined as the branch of international law limiting the use of violence in armed conflicts by:
Sparing those who do not or no longer directly participate in hostilities;
Limiting the violence to the amount necessary to achieve the aim of the conflict, which can be - independently of the cause fought for - only to weaken the military potential of the enemy?

This body of law sets out detailed rules aimed at protecting the victims of armed conflict and restricting the means and methods of warfare. So, law of armed conflict is a collection of;

those international rules, established by treaty or custom, which are specifically intended to solve humanitarian problems directly arising from armed conflicts and which, for humanitarian reasons, limit the right to parties to a conflict to use methods and means of warfare of their choice or protect persons and property that are, or may be, affected by the conflict.

The primary objective of this genre of law is to protect the human being and to safeguard the dignity of man in the extreme situation of war. Its provisions have always been tailored to fit human requirements, and they are bound to an ideal. The protection of man from the consequences of brute force is the objective of this body of law. This law is generally regarded as jus in bello as it regulates the conduct of armed conflict after parties involved have already resorted to force. It is different from the body of law which stipulates circumstances when war is permissible which is regarded as jus ad bellum. The law of armed conflict is applicable in two situations, that is to say, it offers two systems of protection:

International armed conflict: in such situations the Geneva Conventions and Additional Protocol I apply. This body of laws applies to conflicts between two sovereign and independent states engaged in an armed confrontation. They also apply to wars of national liberation, as defined in Article I of Protocol I. Common Article 2 to the Geneva Conventions of 1946 states that:

In addition to the provisions which shall be implemented in peacetime, the present Convention shall apply to all cases of declared war or of any other armed conflict which may arise between two or more of the High contracting Parties, even if the state of war is not recognized by one of them. The Convention shall also apply to all cases of partial occupation of the territory of a High Contracting Party, even if the said occupation meets with no resistance.

The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) proposed a general definition of international armed conflict. In the Tadic case, the tribunal stated that “an armed conflict exists whenever there is a resort to armed force between states”.
H. P. Gasser explains that:

any use of armed force by one state against the territory of another, triggers the acceptability of the Geneva Conventions between the two states. It is also of no concern whether or not the party attacked resisted. As soon as the armed forces of one state find themselves with wounded or surrendering members of the armed forces or the civilians of another state on the other hands, as soon as they detain prisoners or have actual control over a part of the territory of the enemy state, then they must comply with the relevant convention.

Non - International armed conflict: Here, Article 3 Common to the four Geneva Conventions and Additional Protocol II apply. It should be noted that the conditions of application of Additional Protocol II are stricter than those provided for by Article 3. In such situations, humanitarian law is intended for the armed forces, whether regular or not, taking part in the conflict, and protects every individual or category of individuals not or no longer actively involved in the hostilities. Since 2009, the Nigerian nation has been under siege as a result of armed confrontation by Islamist insurgent movement Jama'atu Sunnah Lida Awali Wali Jihad, popularly called Boko Haram. Boko Haram was founded by Yobe State born Muslim Cleric called Mohammed Yusuf in 2002 with its headquarters in Maiduguri, Borno State. Boko Haram is not only opposed to interaction with the Western World which it forbids in its teaching, it is also against the Muslim establishment and government of Nigeria. However in 2009 at the demise of their founder, their aim was geared towards the Islamization of the entire country, Nigeria.

In 2012, tensions within Boko Haram resulted in the gradual split of the group between Salafist conservative faction led by Abu Usmatul al - Ansari, and the more dominant violent faction led by Abubakar shekau. By 2015, part of the group split into al-Qaeda affiliated Ansaru, and Shekau's faction became ISIL's West African branch. Boko Haram militants attacked several Nigerian towns in the Northeast and in their trail left several deaths and destructions of property and even establishing government in towns like Bama, Gwoza, Mandara and Mongunu. A coalition of military forces led by Nigeria which include Chad, Cameroon and Niger began a counter insurgency campaign against them. In the wake of this military 'revenge', there was massive state murder and wanton destruction of civilian property which gives no military advantage to the federal forces. The properties destroyed were not regarded as military necessity or military objectives. The Nigeria military forces committed various violations, war crimes and crimes against humanity.

Armed conflict in the Northeast is a development issue. This war killed but the consequences extend far beyond these direct deaths. In addition to battlefield casualties, this armed conflict led to forced migration, refugee inflows, internally displaced, capital flight, and destruction of the state's infrastructure. Social, political and economic institutions in Borno, Yobo and Adamawa States have been indelibly harmed. The consequences of this conflict for development are staggering and profound.

This paper will evaluate the term armed conflict and the conflict in the Northeast, its prosecution by the Nigerian state, compliance with the law of armed conflict and the consequent effect of this armed conflict to the development of Nigeria especially the Northeast.
